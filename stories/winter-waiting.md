His desk was crowded as it always was. Fly boxes sat half-open in uneven stacks, the task lamp at eye-level illuminating every drift of dubbing and feather that scattered the maple top. His laptop was being used for nothing more than some classic rock; background noise.

To his right, nearly a dozen rod tubes leaned against the wall, some open, some not, with a couple of assembled rods next to them. One was an Orvis seven-weight—perfect for the salmon runs in the area. The other, his father's Scott G, was the one he always reached for first.

## Morning Water

The first time he'd cast it, he knew it was something special. His father had taken him out before sunrise, when the black spruce along the water still looked like part of the dark itself. Trout rose in the pond around them as if the whole surface were quietly boiling.

> Remember, don't force it. Just a smooth, easy motion.

A few false casts and a single clean loop had been enough. Since then, every winter he returned to the same gestures at the same desk, as though a season could be tied down with thread and feather if his hands were steady enough.

The storm outside tapped at the siding all afternoon. By dusk the windows had become mirrors, and in them he kept seeing another room behind him—older, narrower, lit by the same lamp in a place that no longer existed.

When he turned, there was only the wall, the rods, and the long patience of winter.
